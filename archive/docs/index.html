Measured Stress

Jessica E. Snyder
August 2021

Welcome.

This project researches ways to use technology to help people manage pain, so medical staff have alternatives to opiates and consequently less people become newly addicted to their prescriptions. Opiate substitutes must fulfill the essential functions of drugs to soothe, sedate, anesthetize, and relax. Technology could match the desired emotional effect of drugs with virtual or augmented reality (VR/AR). The objective is to engineer and validate pain mitigation strategies based on VR/AR that guides participants towards calmer emotional states using biofeedback from wearable sensors.
The broader impact of this work diversifies options for pain management, which alleviates the pressure on opiates as the workhorse solution and reduces the risk of chemical dependency. VR/AR immerses participants in stimulus-rich worlds designed to overload cognitive function. Gaming or navigating requires significant mental bandwidth for decision making, which has been shown to rate-limit the processing of pain propagating from the body during a procedure or therapy. If the patient feels less pain, then they are better able to comply with rehabilitation practices. Relaxing experiences could alleviate agitation during infusion therapies like chemotherapy, dialysis, or sickle cell treatment. VR/AR could be a new tool to solve problems in the hospital like boredom, education, and social isolation. If portable, patients could bring distraction technology home for acute instances of pain. Technology carries its own risks for addiction that must be carefully surveyed. The intellectual merit is an objective measure of emotion collected using wearable sensors that generates VR/AR experiences designed to match the emotional effect of drugs. The biofeedback builds an archive of data useful for studying the energy generation and dissipation in the central nervous system. This information can be used to build better tissue engineering tools for researchers that model neural circuits.
